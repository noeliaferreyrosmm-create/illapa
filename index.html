01 — The Problem

El Niño is not unpredictable. It is unmeasured.

Description of the Issue

El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) is a recurring climate pattern in which warm Pacific Ocean water shifts eastward, hitting Peru's northern coast directly. For Chiclayo — capital of the Lambayeque region — this means catastrophic flooding every 3 to 7 years, followed by severe droughts in the same agricultural zones. The Reque, Chancay, and Zaña rivers overflow. Informal urban settlements flood with zero warning. Rice, sugarcane, and asparagus fields — the economic backbone of the region — collapse. Disease outbreaks of dengue, leptospirosis, and cholera follow. And then, just as communities begin to rebuild, the cycle starts again.

The brutal irony is that El Niño is a predictable phenomenon. The Pacific Ocean sends measurable signals weeks before a flood event reaches Chiclayo. The problem is not that we cannot know — it is that no system exists to read those signals in time and communicate them to communities, farmers, and city authorities.

Causes and Consequences of the Problem

Root Causes

🌡️
Climate Change amplifying ENSO

Rising global temperatures increase Pacific Ocean heat. El Niño cycles become more extreme and less predictable. Peru's northern coast receives a disproportionate climate impact.

🏗️
Critically vulnerable urban infrastructure

Chiclayo's drainage systems were designed for normal rainfall only. Over 60% of urban growth happened informally, without flood planning or resilient building codes.

📡
Zero AI-powered early warning

Peru's national alert system relies on manual meteorological readings. No machine learning model exists for district-level flood prediction. Warnings arrive 24 to 48 hours before peak flood — too late to evacuate safely.

📚
Critical gap in community climate literacy

Schools do not teach El Niño preparedness or evacuation protocols. Families in flood-risk zones do not know their risk level. No accessible digital tool exists for citizens to check climate risk.

→

Consequences

🏠
Mass urban displacement

Informal settlements (pueblos jóvenes) flood completely. Families lose homes, possessions, and livelihoods overnight. In 2017, 1.2 million people were affected nationally, with Chiclayo among the hardest hit.

🌾
Agricultural and economic collapse

Rice, sugarcane, and asparagus — Chiclayo's economic backbone — are destroyed by flooding. Drought phases then destroy what floods did not reach. Smallholder farmers enter debt cycles with no recovery support.

🦟
Post-flood public health crisis

Standing floodwater becomes a vector for dengue, leptospirosis, cholera, and skin diseases. Hospital infrastructure is damaged or overwhelmed. Children and elderly face the highest mortality risk.

📉
Infrastructure destruction and development setback

Roads, bridges, schools, and hospitals are damaged or destroyed. Economic recovery takes 3 to 5 years. Cities never fully rebuild before the next El Niño cycle begins.

Engineering

ILLAPA-DRONE Network

During the engineering workshop, we programmed drones in DroneBlocks to execute geometric flight paths — vertical ascent forming a triangle vertex, horizontal grid displacement, and controlled descent. These three movements map directly to ILLAPA's three phases of climate response. With humidity, temperature, and atmospheric pressure sensors integrated, the system deploys three specialized eco-drone types:

Sentinel Drone — Detect. Rises in a spiral over the Reque, Chancay, and Zaña rivers. Captures real-time water level and soil saturation data. Transmits to the ILLAPA-AI engine and triggers alerts when rivers reach critical thresholds.
Mapping Drone — Assess. Covers aerial grids over flood-affected urban zones with multispectral cameras. Builds high-resolution damage maps that guide emergency response teams in the first 72 hours after an event.
Reforestation Drone — Recover. Descends progressively over degraded watershed zones during the post-flood drought phase. Seeds native Andean vegetation to restore the water absorption capacity of hillsides, reducing the intensity of the next El Niño cycle.

The same three movements traced in the workshop — rise, expand, descend — are exactly the three phases of climate response that ILLAPA needs: detect, map, and recover.

Mathematics

ILLAPA-ALGO Evacuation Optimizer

We modeled Chiclayo as a weighted network where every street has a flood-risk score updated in real time by the AI engine. Using Dijkstra's shortest path algorithm, ILLAPA calculates the safest evacuation route for every high-risk neighborhood, automatically, 6 weeks before the flood peaks. Our betweenness centrality analysis identified the three most critical intersections in the city: if those points flood, the entire evacuation network collapses. Those are now ILLAPA's priority monitoring points.

Beyond the digital model, ILLAPA proposes a physical component: digital panels placed in central city zones that function as normal city maps year-round. During El Niño alerts, their lights trace evacuation routes, shortest paths, and danger locations — making the algorithm accessible to those who cannot navigate virtually.

03 — STEAM Integration

Five disciplines. One mission.

S

Science

We analyzed 30 years of NASA GISS global temperature anomaly data and SENAMHI Peruvian rainfall records to understand the El Niño signal. We identified which years produced major flood events in Chiclayo, and built the datasets that train our prediction model. Understanding the science of what El Niño is — and why it hits Peru's northern coast with such force — is the intellectual foundation of everything ILLAPA proposes.

T

Technology

The ILLAPA-VR Simulator was built in Delightext as a first-person gamified experience set inside an El Niño flood in Chiclayo. Users face five decision stages where each choice carries real consequences visible in the simulation. The goal is behavioral change through experience — making people understand what to do before the storm arrives, not during it.

E

Engineering

Three eco-drone types were programmed in DroneBlocks using geometric flight paths from our workshop. The vertical ascent, horizontal grid, and controlled descent movements map directly to ILLAPA's detect, map, and recover operational cycle. With integrated environmental sensors, these drones form a continuous monitoring and recovery system for Chiclayo's rivers and watersheds.

A

Arts

The visual identity of ILLAPA is rooted in Andean cosmology. Illapa was the Inca god of thunder and storms — the most respected natural force in the Andes. The Incas did not run from storms. They studied them, respected them, and built civilizations that survived them. Our teal and amber color palette connects water, storm, and alert. Every design choice communicates authority and urgency — and every text exists to educate as much as to present.

M

Mathematics

We modeled Chiclayo's road network as a weighted graph using OpenStreetMap data and Python's NetworkX library. Dijkstra's shortest path algorithm calculates optimal evacuation routes. Betweenness, closeness, and degree centrality analysis identified the three intersections whose flooding would collapse city-wide evacuation. Those are now ILLAPA's priority monitoring nodes — the points where Sentinel Drones are positioned first.

The Science of El Niño

El Niño originates in the central tropical Pacific, where abnormal sea surface warming reverses the cold Humboldt Current and loads Peru's atmosphere with moisture — producing extreme rainfall in the north. Peruvian fishermen first detected it near Paita and named it after Christmas. The Pacific sends this signal weeks in advance. ILLAPA is designed to read it.

05 — SDG Alignment

Four global goals. One local solution.

The ILLAPA Smart Resilience System supports four United Nations Sustainable Development Goals by using technology, education, and urban planning to reduce the impact of climate disasters in vulnerable cities.

SDG 4

Quality Education

The VR simulator and the climate education platform embedded in this website teach students and communities about El Niño risks, disaster preparedness, and climate science. Quality education about climate risk is itself a form of disaster prevention — an informed community responds faster, evacuates more safely, and loses fewer lives.

📚 Target: VR simulator reaches 10,000 students in Chiclayo
SDG 11

Sustainable Cities and Communities

ILLAPA helps cities become more resilient through early warning systems, evacuation route optimization, and infrastructure risk mapping. The digital panel system brings the algorithm to physical public spaces. This transforms Chiclayo from a city that reacts to disasters into one that prepares for them — making it safer, more organized, and more sustainable for all residents.

🏙️ Target: 40% reduction in infrastructure damage per event
SDG 12

Responsible Consumption and Production

AI prediction and drone monitoring protect agriculture and infrastructure by delivering actionable warnings before resources are destroyed. Farmers who receive 6-week forecasts can relocate crops, adjust irrigation, and harvest early — reducing food waste and the unsustainable emergency consumption patterns that follow each disaster cycle.

🌾 Target: 60% reduction in agricultural losses per El Niño event
SDG 13

Climate Action

The entire ILLAPA system is climate adaptation in action. By predicting floods, educating communities, and improving disaster response, the project directly contributes to climate resilience strategies. ILLAPA also documents the climate injustice at the core of this problem: Chiclayo contributes less than 0.01% of global CO₂ emissions yet absorbs catastrophic consequences every 3 to 7 years.

⚡ Target: 70% fewer flood-related casualties

A Note on Climate Justice

Chiclayo contributes less than 0.01% of global CO₂ emissions — yet absorbs catastrophic climate consequences every 3 to 7 years. The communities least responsible for global warming suffer the most from it. ILLAPA exists because of that injustice, and because we believe that technology and education can begin to close that gap.
